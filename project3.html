Factors Influencing Public Waste-Sorting Behavior Among Ho Chi Minh City Residents

An academic study merging the TPB & NAT models, validated using SPSS 20 and SmartPLS 4 / AMOS

🔬 Field: Social & Consumer Behavior Research
👤 Role: Leader
📅 Completed: 10/2025

01 / Research Context

Pressing Context & Theoretical Foundation

Household waste generated in Ho Chi Minh City peaks at ~13,000 tons/day, placing heavy strain on urban infrastructure and the cityscape. Despite existing penalty regulations, waste-sorting behavior in public spaces still faces many barriers. This study builds and tests a structural model of the contributing factors to provide a scientific basis for policymaking.

The team merged two globally recognized academic models: the Theory of Planned Behavior — TPB (Ajzen, 1991) and the Norm Activation Theory — NAT (Schwartz, 1977), proposing a system of 9 core hypotheses (H1–H9) describing the relationships between psycho-social factors and actual waste-sorting behavior.

02 / Data Collection

Data Collection & Screening

The study applied an academic mixed-methods approach (Qualitative – Quantitative – Qualitative). The team conducted a preliminary focus group discussion to refine a questionnaire of 32 observed variables. The formal study collected data through the online platform Khaosatme, gathering 358 responses from residents in high-density districts (District 1, District 3, District 7, and Binh Chanh District). After cleaning, the dataset yielded 295 valid samples for the structural analysis.

03 / Reliability Testing

Advanced Scale Reliability Testing

SPSS 20 was used to assess the internal reliability and validity of the scales. 100% of the item groups exceeded the academic reliability threshold (α > 0.6), with corrected item-total correlations all above 0.3. The Personal Norms scale achieved the highest reliability (α = 0.863).

Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) with Varimax rotation confirmed that all factors achieved a sampling adequacy of 0.5 ≤ KMO ≤ 1 and a highly significant Bartlett's test (Sig. = 0.000 < 0.05), with total variance extracted exceeding 50% in every case.

04 / CFA & SEM Fit

Structural Equation Model (SEM)

Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) was run on AMOS to assess unidimensionality and discriminant validity. After 4 rounds of advanced iterative testing to optimize Average Variance Extracted (AVE), the team made a decisive academic call: fully removing 3 observed variables that failed to meet convergence requirements (K4, ATT5, BEH4). After removal, the model achieved good fit with the urban market data. HTMT indices for all construct pairs were below 0.85, confirming full discriminant validity.

05 / Discoveries

Scientific Findings & Hypothesis Testing

SEM path analysis was run in SmartPLS 4 using bootstrapping with 1,000 resamples to determine p-values at a 5% significance level. Inner VIF values < 3 confirm the model has no multicollinearity issues.

  • Intention (INT) is the core determinant of actual waste-sorting behavior (O = 0.607, p = 0.000).
  • For Intention, Policy Enforcement (O = 0.315) and Personal Norms (O = 0.255) have the strongest effects.
  • H1, H7, and H8 rejected: Incentive Measures showed no significant moderating effect (p = 0.808). Notably, Environmental Concern was not a moderating variable but instead emerged as an independent variable with an exceptionally strong direct effect on Attitude (path coefficient as high as 0.584).

💡 Research Implications — Answering the Research Question The results show that Ho Chi Minh City residents do not sort waste in public places mainly due to a lack of "Intention" to act — not a lack of material incentives (H8 rejected). That intention is reinforced most strongly by personal norms and the level of policy enforcement, while environmental concern exerts an indirect effect by shaping attitude. This suggests that communication campaigns should focus on building "social norms" (everyone does it, so should I) and increasing the visibility of legal regulations in public spaces, rather than relying solely on short-term rewards or incentives — alongside standardizing color-coded/labeled waste-sorting infrastructure.
